When creating a game, the designer is the one who creates the invisible. The designer's work is more of setting the structure, rules, and direction of the game, unlike developers writing codes and designers writing visible results like drawing art. The designer's core role is to provide guidelines for the entire team to move toward the same goal.  
  
## what a designer does  
  
A detailed look at what the designer actually does is as follows.  
  
- Game system and mechanism design  
- UX Flow and UI Configuration  
- Content Design (Story, Quest, Level, etc.)  
- Creating planning documents and communicating between team members  
  
Through all of these processes, the planner clearly communicates what and how the team members should make.  

---
## a problem with a team without a designer  
  
Teams without planners are likely to encounter the following problems during development.  
  
- "How does this work?" → Functionality blocked  
- "What does this UI mean?" → Confusion occurs  
- "Is this the balance?" → Game play imbalance  
  
If you move while looking in different directions, you will inevitably get a fragmented result rather than a single completed game.  

---
## the detailed role of the designer  
  
The planner can be classified into three main categories.  
  
- **Systems planner**: Designed core systems of the game, including combat, growth, economy, etc 
- **Level planner**: Design the overall flow of the level, including map structure, movement, difficulty adjustment, etc  
- **Content planner**: Organize player experiences such as stories, items, quests and more  
  
If it's a small team, one person may have to play all these roles.  

---
## The actual flow of game planning  
  
Planning is a continuous process that begins with a simple idea and extends to concrete documentation. The actual workflow can be divided as follows.  
  
---  
  
### 1. Idea materialization  
  
At first, it is the step of setting the core concept of the game and setting the direction. In this step, the following contents are defined.  
  
- **Establishment of game concept**  
	- Genre examples: Horror / Survival / Loglike / Puzzle Adventure  
	- Theme example: SF Lab / Ruined City / Nightmare World  
	- Key fun elements: psychological fears, brain-based puzzles, AI-based behavioral changes, etc  
  
- **Defining Core Mechanics**  
	- e.g. a system that uses light to suppress monsters, a function that stops time during certain actions, etc  
  
- **Gameplay flow design**  
	- Example:  
		- The player is stuck in the lab.  
		- Power is limited, so you have to solve the puzzle while keeping the light.  
		- Enemy is activated when approaching a particular area.  
		- You have to escape within the time limit.  
  
- **Define objectives and compensation systems**  
	- Example: Story ban, new items, enhancements, etc  
  
---  
  
### 2. Game Design  
  
When the idea is materialized, it goes to the design stage of solving it into a real system. From here on, planning documentation becomes important.  
  
- **Defining the gaming system**  
	- Method of movement: first person / third person / top down / side view  
	- Combat Method: No Combat / Avoidance - Main / Limited Attack  
	- Puzzle system: Environmental interaction, psychological tension included  
  
- **Draft of level design**  
	- Example:  
		- Laboratory 1st Floor: Basic Move and Interaction Tutorial  
		- Laboratory 2nd Floor: Unstable Environment with Periodic Lights Out  
		- Lab B: Final Escape Zone, Powerful Enemy Standby  
  
- **UI/UX Design**  
	- Example: flashlight battery UI, heart rate based tension display, puzzle hint  
  
- **Multi-play elements (if applicable)**  
	- Cooperation Structure: One keeps the light, the other solves the puzzle  
	- Communication method: Near-Field Voice Chat, Using Radio Item  
  
---  
  
### 3. Preparation of a proposal  
  
Organize the designed content in a document so that it can be shared with team members. It is important to organize it systematically by referring to the form used in practice.  
  
- **Game overview**  
	- Title (temporary setting)  
	- Genre and Theme  
	- Key differences: e.g.) AI analyzes heart rate to create fear  
  
- **Detailed description of the gaming system**  
	- Includes functional descriptions, input/output structures, game flow charts, etc  
  
- **Art and Sound Direction**  
	- Visual: Dark colors, strong contrast between light and shadow  
	- Sound: Heartbeat that gets faster as the enemy approaches, etc  
  
- **Distribution of development schedules and roles**  
	- Full Development Period  
	- Specify the role of each team member  
  
---  
  
Game planning is not just to come up with an idea, but to embody it in a feasible structure and organize it in a form that the entire team can understand. The team can have a much more stable direction just by having a planner, and the efficiency of development is greatly increased.
